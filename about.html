Domestic Violence can take many forms

Domestic Violence is a multifaceted issue that has many misunderstandings surrounding it. While a staggering number of women (approximately one in every four) are impacted by domestic violence, so too are men (approximately one in every eight). Fortunately, there are abundant resources available to help those in hurtful environments escape from domestic violence situations. Unfortunately, there isn’t enough awareness about the aftermath of domestic violence: the long-term trauma caused by intimate partner abuse or the pathway to rehabilitate abusers so that they may learn how to manage and prevent abusive tendencies. The hope is to enable both the abused and the abusive to have healthy and safe relationships and break the cycle of violence.


What is Domestic Violence?

The definition of domestic violence, according to Domestic Violence: A Reference Handbook that was written by Margi L. McCue, and commonly accepted by legal professionals is the emotional, physical, psychological, or sexual abuse perpetrated against a person by that person’s spouse, former spouse, partner, former partner, or by the other parent of a minor child. In a June 1994 speech, Secretary of Health and Human Services Donna Shalala referred to domestic violence as terrorism in the home (McCue, 1995). There is a slight difference between the terms battering and abuse, although often used interchangeably. Abuse can be a single instance of domestic violence, whereas a systematic pattern of abuse may escalate over time to battering. Marie Bracciale wrote in her dissertation that it appears [human beings] are most likely to behave aggressively in their most intimate social relationships (Chap 1, page 1) and that in the United States, violent crimes occur more frequently within families than among strangers (Chap 1, page 3).

How to Stop. Interpersonal violence needs to be de-escalated before reaching the point of physical violence: it must be addressed through interpersonal communication strategies that avoid negative communication patterns. For this reason, efforts aimed at preventing marital violence before it occurs must involve the reduction of causal risk factors such as negative communication patterns and the bolstering of protective factors such as healthy commitment and the development of a broader support network (Hartman, et al, 2002). The Prevention and Relationship Enhancement Program (PREP) is a first line of defense against intimate partner abuse as it targets enhancing couples’ communication and conflict resolution skills. Stop Anger and Violence Escalation (SAVE) is a program that educates at risk couples on the consequences of physical aggression in relationships and aims to reduce the couples’ tolerance for such behavior in hopes of encouraging couples to better their conflict resolution and communication skills before resulting to violence. Finally, the Physical Aggression Couples Treatment (PACT) methodology treats already violent couples to help them manage their anger before violence occurs by improving the couples’ communication and conflict resolution skills. The focus is placed on triggers that lead to violent behavior like jealousy, power, and negative communication (Hartman et al, 2002).

Robin's Nest

Robin’s Nest is a nonprofit organization focused on creating safe living situations for victims of domestic violence. Understanding that safety is a very complex concept post-trauma, Robin’s Nest seeks to help victims transition out of abusive relationships and stay out by showing them that they can start living life again, without constant fear, and that there is a community of facilitators wanting to help them along their journey. From creating refresh, clean spaces for victims to live in to sourcing donated furniture and household items to help build a home, Robin’s Nest will be an inviting safe harbor for victims of domestic violence.

Types of Abuse

Emotional and Psychological Abuse

Emotional abuse deals with the psychological aspect of domestic violence in that it creates emotional instability within the victim by targeting insecurities, self-esteem, and or the victim’s self-image. It always accompanies physical abuse and in most cases precedes physical battering (McCue, 1995). Emotional abuse is the psychological terror inflicted by an abuser that makes the victim afraid to leave or causes them to return to abusive relationships.

Sexual Abuse

Sexual abuse occurs when the intimate partner coerces or forces the victim to perform sexual acts that make them uncomfortable or that they do not want to do. Additionally, sexual abuse may take the form of sexual harassment, which is any unwanted physical touch, advances, or language of a sexual nature.

Physical Abuse

Any action that causes physical harm to the victim is considered physical abuse, whether as minor as pinching or squeezing, restraining, or holding to overt acts of physical violence that may potentially lead to death. Additionally, physical abuse may begin in a physically nonviolent way with neglect, which can include failing to meet the victim’s needs for physical intimacy (McCue, 1995).
